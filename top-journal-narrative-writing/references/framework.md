# Top-Journal Narrative Framework

## The difference between “story” and “packaging”

A scientific story is the logical path from an important unresolved problem to a defensible conclusion. It is not dramatic wording, selective reporting, or hiding uncertainty.

A manuscript becomes stronger when the reader can answer these questions quickly:

1. Why does this problem matter beyond the authors’ immediate subfield?
2. What exactly was unknown before this study?
3. Why did previous methods or explanations fail?
4. What did this study do that is decisive?
5. Which figure proves which claim?
6. What changed in the field because of the result?
7. What remains unresolved?

## Four-layer narrative

### 1. Editor layer

The editor asks: Is this sufficiently important, surprising, rigorous, and relevant to the journal’s readership?

Required output: one paragraph that explains the central advance and journal fit without repeating the abstract.

### 2. Reader layer

The broad scientific reader asks: What is the problem, why should I care, and what did they find?

Required output: title, abstract, opening paragraph, and figure order that are intelligible without deep subfield knowledge.

### 3. Specialist layer

The expert reviewer asks: Is the novelty real, are methods appropriate, are controls sufficient, and are claims calibrated?

Required output: claim-evidence map, methods transparency, comparison with closest prior work, alternative explanations.

### 4. Future-use layer

The field asks: Can this be reused, extended, cited, reproduced, or challenged?

Required output: clear limitations, data/code/material availability where relevant, and precise definitions.

## Rubric

| Score | Central claim | Evidence | Significance | Reader clarity | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5 | One precise field-moving claim | Direct, decisive, replicated or triangulated | Broad and specific | Clear to non-specialists | Low |
| 4 | Clear claim with minor ambiguity | Strong but one gap remains | Important to adjacent fields | Mostly clear | Moderate |
| 3 | Claim exists but competes with subclaims | Evidence scattered | Mostly subfield-level | Jargon-heavy | Desk-review risk |
| 2 | Descriptive catalogue | Indirect or undercontrolled | Incremental | Hard to follow | High reviewer risk |
| 1 | No stable claim | Unsupported or inconsistent | Unclear | Inaccessible | Reject-level risk |

## Story-spine template

Although [field/problem] is central to [broader context], it remains unclear whether/how [specific unknown] because [barrier or limitation]. Here we [approach] and show that [main finding]. This finding [mechanism/principle] and [field-level implication], while [boundary/limitation].

## Claim calibration ladder

- “Proves”: almost never use in empirical manuscripts.
- “Demonstrates/establishes”: use when causal or decisive evidence is direct.
- “Shows/reveals”: use for strong direct results.
- “Supports/indicates”: use for strong but not definitive evidence.
- “Suggests/is consistent with”: use for exploratory, correlative, or limited evidence.
- “May/could”: use for hypotheses, implications, or future work.
