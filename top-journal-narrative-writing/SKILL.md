# Top-Journal Narrative Writing

## Purpose

Help the user turn a research result into a clear, rigorous, editor-readable manuscript narrative. Optimize for scientific importance, broad readability, evidence traceability, and claim calibration—not hype or cosmetic “storytelling.”

## Non-negotiable rules

1. Never invent data, citations, experimental details, limitations, journal policies, author contributions, or reviewer comments.
2. Do not upgrade weak evidence into strong claims. Mark unsupported claims explicitly.
3. Preserve the user's scientific meaning unless asked to challenge it.
4. Separate “what the data show” from “what the authors infer.”
5. Check target-journal instructions when current requirements matter and tools allow web access. If not verified, label journal-specific formatting as “to verify.”
6. If AI use affects journal compliance, advise the user to follow the target journal’s current AI disclosure policy.
7. Prefer concise, concrete, falsifiable statements over adjectives such as “novel,” “groundbreaking,” “paradigm-shifting,” or “unprecedented.”

## When starting a task

If the user provides a manuscript, abstract, outline, or figure list, proceed directly. If key information is missing, infer cautiously and state assumptions rather than blocking. Useful inputs include:

- Target journal or journal family.
- Field and article type.
- One-sentence main claim.
- Figure list or key evidence.
- Competing explanation or closest prior work.
- Audience: general scientist, field specialist, reviewer, editor, grant panel.
- Word limits, if known.

## Core model: the scientific story spine

Use this spine to diagnose or rebuild the manuscript:

1. **Context:** What field-level problem matters beyond the immediate subfield?
2. **Known:** What has already been established?
3. **Unknown or tension:** What important gap, contradiction, limitation, or bottleneck remains?
4. **Barrier:** Why has this not been solved before?
5. **Approach:** What decisive method, dataset, theory, model, or experiment breaks the barrier?
6. **Main finding:** What is the central result in one sentence?
7. **Evidence chain:** Which figures/results prove the claim, and in what order?
8. **Mechanism/principle:** What general rule, mechanism, or causal explanation emerges?
9. **Implication:** How does this move the field forward?
10. **Boundary:** Where does the claim not yet apply?

A good top-journal narrative usually has one dominant contribution, not a catalogue of results.

## Standard workflow

### 1. Diagnose before rewriting

Produce a short diagnostic table with these dimensions:

- Central claim clarity.
- Importance beyond subfield.
- Gap and tension.
- Novelty relative to closest prior work.
- Figure/evidence logic.
- Claim-evidence calibration.
- Accessibility for non-specialists.
- Risk of desk rejection or reviewer objection.

Use labels: `Strong`, `Needs work`, or `Risk`.

### 2. Build a claim-evidence map

For every major claim, identify:

- Claim text.
- Supporting figure/table/result.
- Strength of evidence: direct, indirect, correlative, exploratory, computational, theoretical, qualitative.
- Missing control, comparison, validation, or limitation.
- Safer wording if evidence is not decisive.

### 3. Reconstruct the figure-first narrative

Before rewriting prose, create a figure-story map:

- Figure 1: Establish phenomenon, system, dataset, or central observation.
- Figure 2: Demonstrate core effect or main quantitative result.
- Figure 3: Reveal mechanism, causal path, model, or explanatory principle.
- Figure 4: Validate, perturb, benchmark, replicate, or generalize.
- Figure 5: Show broader consequence, application, cross-context transfer, or boundary condition.
- Supplementary figures: robustness, parameter sweeps, secondary analyses, extra controls.

Adapt this order to the actual field; do not force figures into a false template.

### 4. Rewrite section by section

#### Title

Generate 3 options:

1. **Descriptive:** specific object + finding.
2. **Mechanistic:** mechanism/principle + system.
3. **Significance-led:** field-level consequence + evidence base.

Avoid vague labels, unsupported superlatives, unexplained acronyms, and “first-ever” claims unless proven.

#### Abstract or summary paragraph

Use a 5-move structure:

1. Broad context in one sentence.
2. Specific unresolved gap or tension.
3. Approach or enabling evidence.
4. Main result, preferably with concrete quantitative or mechanistic detail when available.
5. Field-level implication plus boundary/condition if needed.

Do not include citations unless the target journal requires or allows them. Avoid dense methods detail unless essential to credibility.

#### Introduction

Prefer 4 compact paragraphs:

1. **Why this problem matters:** broad context and stakes.
2. **What is known and what fails:** closest prior work, limitation, contradiction, or bottleneck.
3. **The missing piece:** precise gap and why resolving it matters now.
4. **Here we show:** one-sentence contribution, approach, and paper roadmap.

The final paragraph should make the editor understand both “why now?” and “why this journal?”

#### Results

For each subsection:

1. Start with the question or rationale.
2. State what was tested or analyzed.
3. Present the result with enough quantitative specificity.
4. Explain why the result matters for the central claim.
5. End with a takeaway that sets up the next subsection.

Avoid turning Results into Methods. Put routine detail in Methods or supplementary material when appropriate.

#### Discussion

Use this order unless the field dictates otherwise:

1. Direct answer to the question posed in the Introduction.
2. What principle/mechanism/concept has changed.
3. Comparison with closest prior work, including conflicting evidence.
4. Implications for the field or adjacent fields.
5. Limitations and boundary conditions.
6. Specific next step only if it follows naturally.

Do not repeat the abstract or overextend beyond evidence.

#### Cover letter or presubmission inquiry

Use 4 short paragraphs:

1. One-sentence central discovery.
2. Why the advance is important, timely, and field-moving.
3. Why the target journal’s readership should care.
4. Compliance notes: related submissions, conflicts, reviewer suggestions/exclusions if requested, data/code availability if relevant.

Do not merely copy the abstract.

### 5. Calibrate language

Use stronger verbs only when evidence is direct.

- Direct causal evidence: “demonstrates,” “shows,” “establishes,” “reveals.”
- Strong association or converging evidence: “supports,” “indicates,” “is consistent with.”
- Exploratory or limited evidence: “suggests,” “raises the possibility,” “motivates.”
- Avoid unless proven: “proves,” “settles,” “revolutionizes,” “first,” “universal.”

### 6. Produce useful outputs

When revising text, return:

1. **Rewritten version** ready to paste.
2. **Why these edits help** in 3–6 bullets.
3. **Risk notes**: overclaim, unsupported gap, missing comparison, unclear figure logic, jargon, or journal-fit concern.
4. **Next revision actions** prioritized by impact.
